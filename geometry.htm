Go-Karts

The reference frame

Imagine Isaac Newton has a go-kart, and he places a red apple on the track every one second. If he's traveling at a consistent 1 m/s, the apples will be evenly spaced 1 m apart.

From Newton's frame of reference, the go-kart is not moving, and it appears as if the apples are being propelled backwards from it.

Both frames of reference are equally valid. If Newton was in a perfectly-suspended windowless enclosure on the go-kart, would he know if the kart is in motion or at rest? Would he know if he was traveling in a straightline or along a gentle curve?

With those age-old questions in mind, think about the ticks of a stationary clock. On one hand, it is intuitive to think of the clock as moving forward in time, leaving behind a trail of spacetime events representing its ticks. The clock moves into the future, leaving the past behind.

Alternatively, and perhaps less intuitively, consider the view from the clock's frame of reference: it is always in the present; it never leaves and the future never arrives; events that occur in the present get pushed into the past, which itself is moving away from the present. To visualize this view of time, it may be helpful to imagine the observer as being on a go-kart that is on a conveyor belt moving against it.

Is it possible to make a measurement or observation that would distinguish one noton of time from the other, or are they equivalent? Is it possible that cosmic time dilation and redshift are evidence of one view over the other?

The geometry of time

To answer the questions of the previous section, and to provide the geometrical foundations for a hypothesis, the unusual step of giving curvature to time will be taken. Note that this is not curvature in the Gaussian sense, which is the product of two principals of curvature. Since time is one dimensional, it only has the opportunity to curve in one direction.

The simplest example of curved time is in a circle. So consider Newton in his go-kart, which is placed on a circular conveyor belt that matches his speed in the opposite direction.

Were Newton and the conyeor belt to take a break after an appropriate amount of time, he would notice upon surveying the track that the distance to each apple from himself is no longer evenly spaced. After half a rotation of the track, the apple's begin to bunch together, and then start heading back toward Newton. When Newton resumes his driving, sure enough, he'll see the apple's he had left behind coming toward him from the front.

Typically we would say that time is the real number line, or formally, that \(t\in\mathbb{R}\). Here we are presented with an alternative option, where our dimension of time is on the circumference of a 2-dimensional circle, or \(t\in\mathbb{S}^1\), where \( \mathbb{S}^1 = \{ (x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x^2 + y^2 = R^2 \} \).

The distance along the curve, we'll call \(\tau\), is \(\tau = R\theta\), and the distance in the 2-d ambient space that Newton measures, which we'll call \(t\), is given by the Pythagorean theorem:

\[ t^2 = (R \cos( \frac{\tau}{R}) - R)^2 + (R \sin(\frac{\tau}{R}))^2 \]

In the above equation, \(t\) will be the time coordinate in the resulting dimension of time, which represents the length of a chord on the circle, thus preserving the distance in the ambient space.

If time is the surface of a circle as described here, the events that happen in the present will be bunched together in the past. This would lead to the observation of time contraction and blueshifted light. Since we observe the opposite, let's apply the same reasoning to a hyperbolic track:

Now in the ambient space, the more distant the events, the more spread out they become, which is observed as time dilation and redshift.

The same reasoning applies as before; our \(t\) coordinate will be the length of a chord of the hyperbola from the starting position, which can be found from the arc length, \(\tau\):

\[t^2 = (R \cosh(\frac{\tau}{R}) - R)^2 + (R \sinh (\frac{\tau}{R}))^2 \]

This function gives us the lookback time from the time given by the arc length. The arc length \(\tau\) represents time without the effects of time dilation. Therefore \(c\tau\) gives you the physical distance to the source, and \(ct\) gives you the light travel time distance.

At z=1, the effects of time dilation should double the duration of some kind of cosmic event. However, the lookback time will not be double. This is because the light travels from z=1 to z=0 and passes all the points along the way. To find how much time dilation is occuring at a certain distance, we need to find the slope of the above equation for the appropriate \(\tau\).

\[ 1 + z = \frac{\text{d}t}{\text{d}\tau} = \frac{\cosh{\frac{3 \tau}{2 R} } }{\sqrt{\cosh{ \frac{\tau}{R}}}} \]

Our measurements give us the \(t\) coordinate (the chord length), so in the cases of finding \(\tau\) (the arc length) we can use the reverse:

\[ \tau = R \cosh^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{ \frac{2t^2}{R^2} + 1} +\frac{1}{2} \right)\]
