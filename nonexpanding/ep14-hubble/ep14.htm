Edwin Hubble


"Some time after Pearl Harbor, while I was trying to get back into the Infantry, I was called in by Army Ordnance and told, in effect, to stop that nonsense, that I had been ear-marked long before for a job in Ordnance.... My name, they told me, was top of the list because ballistics had a curious affinity with astronomy...


I rushed to a dictionary. Ballistics, it seemed, is the science of hurling missiles against an enemy. This seemed a trifle thin so I turned to the encyclopaedia; there I found a long article, but it puzzled me. Ballistics was either underdeveloped or highly classified.
I found out Ballistics was both underdeveloped and highly classified."

He got a medal of merit in 1946 for his research.

After the war, Hubble sat on the advisory board of the 200-inch Hale telescope.

It surpassed the 100-inch telescope at Mt. Wilson as the world's largest telescope.

In 1949, Hubble was the inagural observer of the telescope.

Which is named after his former boss, George Ellery Hale.

Hale originally invited Hubble to work at Mt. Wilson in 1917
as Hubble was finishing his doctorate.

The US had just entered WWI.

Rushing through his thesis, Hubble joined the Army and sent Hale a telegraph:





"Regret cannot accept your invitation. Am off to the war."

Hubble returned from the war and accepted a position at Mt. Wilson in 1919.

Meanwhile...

    

The Debate:

Are these "nebulae" clouds of gas and dust in the Milky Way?

Or separate, distant groups of stars?

The Milky Way was commonly thought to be the whole universe

Though, some thought the Milky Way was one of these "island universes"

Hubble ended the debate in 1924, by observing Cepheid variable stars in Andromeda

Using their properties a distance measurement could be made

Andromeda was in fact a very distant system of stars

Nebulae were no doubt the "true inhabitants of space."

Today we know them as galaxies

By 1929, Hubble measured the distances to more galaxies

And with the redshifts measured by Vesto Slipher's spectroscopy


Found a correlation in their apparent motion

The farther away a galaxy is...

The faster it's moving away from us.



Due to the principle that we aren't in a special place in the universe:

If everything's getting farther away from us...

... then everything is getting farther away from everything.

The universe must be expanding.

This meant that Edwin Hubble discovered that the universe was expanding

... but not if you asked Edwin Hubble.



Page 47
"The familiar interpretation of red-shifts as velocity-shifts very seriously restricts not only the time scale, the age of the universe, but the spatial dimensions as well. On the other hand, the alternative possible interpretation, that red-shifts are not velocity-shifts, avoids both difficulties, and presents the observable region as an insignificant sample of a universe that extends indefinitely in space and in time."



Page 52
"Two pictures of the universe are sharply drawn. Observations, at the moment, seem to favour one picture, but they do not rule out the other. We seem to face, as once before in the days of Copernicus, a choice between a small, finite universe, and a universe indefinitely large plus a new principle of nature."

Notes:

At this time, based on early measurements

The estimated age of the universe was only ~2 billion years

Less than the age of the Earth

So restriction of the time-scale was far more severe than it is today



But the problem really never went away...

Though Hubble rejected it, expansion was accepted by the new field of cosmology



Even the Big Bang's most well known competition in the 1950's,
the "Steady State Theory", was an expanding universe theory

Though new voices and ideas in cosmology took hold,
