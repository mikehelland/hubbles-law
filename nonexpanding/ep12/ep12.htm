Do quasar jets produce fresh hydrogen?

  • Hydrogen is the simplest and lightest atom
  • Its nucleus is 1 proton
  • Hydrogen makes up 75% of the universe's atoms
  • It was created exclusively in the big bang

Not all the original hydrogen from the big bang is left

Some of it has been fused into heavier elements

Fusion creates heavier elements up until iron, then hits a dead-end

Fission can break heavier elements like lead and gold apart

But fission hits a dead-end at iron too

(link in the description)

Everything goes to iron...




More importantly, nothing goes back to hydrogen.

Hydrogen hasn't been around since the very beginning

 

It took a few microseconds after the big bang before protons and neutrons could form

 

Protons and neutrons are made of quarks

Back then it was too hot for the quarks to stick together

(img source: National Geographic)

But it quickly cooled, and protons and neutrons formed

(img source: National Geographic)

And if you have protons...



You have hydrogen nuclei!



(a.k.a., Positively-charged hydrogen ions)

So how hot is too hot for protons and neutrons?

A boiling point of 1.6 TRILLION K!

So whether it's a single proton like hydrogen...

Or almost a hundred protons like lead...

With enough pressure and heat...

The nuclei (and their protons and neutrons) will "boil" ...


... into something called Quark-Gluon Plamsa (QGP)

Is any of this real?

Yes!

Quark-Gluon Plasma has been created in labs!

By ramming heavy nuclei like gold and lead together in colliders (LHC and RHIC)...

Temperatures are hot enough to produce QGP!

When these nuclei collide, they boil into QGP

And you even get these little jets.

Ok, so what's that got to do with quasars?

Quasars are powerful galaxy centers with big jets!

And, in 2016, these jets have been measured to be 10 trillion K

That's hotter than the boiling point of nuclei!

So what if some atom, like lead, comes into contact with these jets?

Wouldn't it behave the same way it does in labs, and after the big bang?


  • The atom turns into quarks...
  • Then cools into protons and neutrons...
  • Then...

We're back to hydrogen!

Do quasar jets produce "fresh" hydrogen?

It seems like they could.
