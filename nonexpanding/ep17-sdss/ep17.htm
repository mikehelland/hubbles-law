The cosmological parameters are:

$H_0, \Lambda_\Omega, \Lambda_m$

Hubble's constant, dark energy and matter densities

The Hubble constant ($H_0$) used in DR16 is $67.6$ km/s/Mpc

These parameters famously over-predict the supernovae data at the same redshfits

This choice of parameters places objects farther away, thus increasing their brightness

But not by a significant amount.

A few other factors could effect measured brightness:

  • Gravitational lensing
  • Double quasars
  • Interloping starlight
  • Intrinsic/gravitational redshift

Gravitational Lensing

The quasar's brightness was increased by lensing.

Double Quasars

Interloping stars

There are hundreds of billions of stars in our galaxy, and hundreds of billions of other galaxies.

There are only about 1 million known quasars.

The odds aren't low a star or galaxy would fall in a quasars line of sight to us.

Gravitational redshift?

If a quasar's light originates near a super massive blackhole

Part of their redshift could be from their original high-gravity enrivonment

Meaning they are not as distant, and thus, not as bright as measured.

All of these factors combined could skew the data.
