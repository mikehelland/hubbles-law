Fixing redshift

Is redshift broken?

Well. Not exactly.

We use $z$ to represent redshift and ...

I'll just show you

Standard Model of Cosmology

redshift
$1+z = \frac{\text{wavelength}_{now}}{\text{wavelength}_{then}}$

 

"scale factor"
$a= \frac{1}{1+z} = \frac{\text{wavelength}_{then}}{\text{wavelength}_{now}}$

$a = 0.5$ and the half way point don't line up exactly

because gravity and dark energy play "tug-of-war" with the expansion rate.

You can't avoid that...

... in the standard model of cosmology.

Alternative Model of Cosmology (from Ep. 8)

redshift and radius
$1-r = a = \frac{1}{1+z} = \frac{\text{wavelength}_{then}}{\text{wavelength}_{now}}$

Remember

Light has wavelength and frequency.

And energy.

$r = 1 - a = \frac{z}{1+z}$


Examples

  • $z = 0.5$
  • $a = 0.667$
  • $r = 33\%$

    • which means:

  • Light is 33% redshifted
  • (missing $\frac{1}{3}$ of its energy)
  • Distance is 33% of $\frac{c}{H}$
  • $z = 3$
  • $a = 0.25$
  • $r = 75\%$

    • which means:

  • Light is 75% redshifted
  • (missing $\frac{3}{4}$ of its energy)
  • Distance is 75% of $\frac{c}{H}$

Alternative model

Summary

  • Redshift given by $z$, $a$, or $r$
  • All are valid. The choice doesn't change anything
  • Standard Model
    • the scale factor $a$ is the most natural
  • de Sitter universe
    • $z$ and $r$ give exact distances
  • Alternative model
    • Only $r$ gives the exact distance

Conclusion

  • If we do switch from the standard model to the alternative...
  • ... it would be convenient to switch from $z$ to $r$ too.
