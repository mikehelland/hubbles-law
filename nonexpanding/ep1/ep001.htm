Part 1

Redshift




Light is an electromagnetic wave




It has:
  • wavelength \(\lambda\)
  • period \(t\)
  • frequency \(f\)
\[ f = \frac{1}{t} \]
                        

Redshift by wavelength

\[ \text{wavelength} ~\lambda \]
\[ \lambda_{emit} = \text{400 nm}\]
400 nm
\[ \lambda_{obs} = \text{600 nm}\]
600 nm
\[ 1 + z = \frac{\lambda_{obs}}{\lambda_{emit}}\]
\[ z = \frac{\text{600 nm}}{\text{400 nm}} - 1\]
\[ z = 0.5 \]

Wavelength and Frequency




Frequency × wavelength is the wave's speed, which is \(c\)

\[ c = f\lambda \]

When wavelength goes up, frequency goes down

\[ f = \frac{c}{\lambda} \]

Redshift by frequency

\[ \text{frequency} ~ f\]
\[ f_{emit} = \text{750 THz}\]
750 THz
\[ f_{obs} = \text{500 THz}\]
500 THz
\[ 1 + z = \frac{f_{emit}}{f_{obs}}\]
\[ z = \frac{\text{750 THz}}{\text{500 THz}} - 1\]
\[ z = 0.5 \]

The Time Dilated Past Hypothesis




Observations

  • Redshift
  • Time dilation

Interpretation

Redshifted light is a time dilated EM wave

Conjecture

The past is time dilated.




Assumption: Without time dilation, spacetime would be Minkowski

With time dilation, events are more spread out

The time coordinates

  • Measured time, \(t\), shows time dilation
  • Minkowski time, \(\tau\), no time dilation

Measured-to-Minkowski time

\[ \tau = \frac{1}{H_0} (e^{H_0 t} - 1) \]

Minkowski-to-Measured time

\[ t = \frac{1}{H_0} \log (H_0 \tau + 1) \]
    

(Link to paper in the description)

Redshift-luminosity distance relationship

\(d_L = \frac{c}{H_0} (1+z) \log (1+z) \)

\(\mu = 5 \log_{10}d_L + 25 \)


Compared to the Pantheon+SH0ES Supernovae data

To find the value of H0 with the best fit

Sum of Squared Errors (SSE)

\( \chi^2= \Sigma_i (\mu_{i}^d - \mu_{i}^m)^2 / \sigma^2_i \)


  • \(\mu_{i}^d = \) distance modulus in the data
  • \(\mu_{i}^m = \) distance modulus in the model from the data's redshift
  • \(\sigma^2_i = \) error margin
  • \(\mu = 5 \log_{10}d_L + 25 \)
  • \(d_L = \frac{c}{H_0} (1+z) \log (1+z) \)

Calculate \(\chi^2\) over a range of values for H0

A lower \(\chi^2\) is a better fit
